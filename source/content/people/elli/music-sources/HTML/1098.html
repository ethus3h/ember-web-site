What I'm Reading/Listening to on CD. . .

April 29: Wow. I browsed through a bunch of books and then settled into a few, but I am way behind in my reviews! So: in the car I am listening to Rick Riordan's The Lightning Thief, which would be perfect for my Mythology class. I also relistened to The Inimitable Jeeves just before break. I love Wodehouse!
    At the very end of vacation, I read Betty Carter's Swim to Me, which started out well and ended up . . . meh; am reading both Michael Phelps's interesting but poorly written autobio No Limits and Carter (?)'s bio of Harper Lee, Mockingbird--excellent so far. Today I was hijacked by a book Anne S gave me: The Hedgehog's Dilemma, a wonderful British natural history book by a hedgehog nut with a great sense of humor. There has been more! However, I did a lot of "tasting" reading, finding various books not worth the effort--most notably some Jemima Shore mysteries by Antonia Fraser. Book sale fodder!
Mar. 28: Here's a shout out to those Twilight fans amongst you: try Mary Stewart. Not her Arthurian ones (though maybe you'll like those, too) but This Rough Magic. Nine Coaches Waiting. The Moonspinners. My latest reread, Wildfires at Midnight. They're old, classic, well-written, atmospheric "gothic romances"--note the "well-written"--I understand Twilight is not. . . . Right now I am between books, and feeling antsy about it. Nearly done listening to Persuasion as I sew, but when I flop down exhausted at the end (or the middle) of the day (darn cold!), I don't have a go-to book. Need one!
     I power-read Paul Rusesebagina's An Ordinary Man as background for our Social Responsibility Day and watching "Hotel Rwanda", and I'd recommend that for sure. Not always pleasant reading, but important.
    Also browsed through Ann Brashares's new offering after the Traveling Pants series: Three Willows. Did not work for me. The writing seemed really choppy and uneven, and the whole intertwined friends story. . . well, she's done it before, and it seems really obvious that she's starting these girls younger so she can get a few more books out of their lives!
    In the car I am slogging through the last four cds of Middlesex. Right now, too much information! Too many cutesy metaphors about stuff we don't need to have described, thank you very much. But I am going to finish and see if I can see the driving reason for this enormous book at the end.
    Well, that's a pretty negative entry. Can you tell it's late March and the dirty snow and dead grass is getting to me????????? And my lingering cold? But I promise I am only one good book, one good nap, one inspiring knitting project, away from complete and total enthusiasm about everything. I promise!

Mar. 22: Have read a lot but not posted. Finished A Perfect Life and found it pretty good, though my sense is that it is very autobiographical and it makes me curious!
Also reread Jennifer Weiner's Certain Girls, which was not as good as her In Her Shoes, but did make me cry (again), and read a Katie Fforde fluff book which was, nonetheless, fun. AND lots of magazines, as I suddenly have three subscriptions! I know there were at least two others, but I can't visualize them right now. Oh: Marian Keyes's The Other Side of the Story, which is a lot of fun--it's Irish chick lit--so something different! And my other elusive title came to me this morning: The Shuttle, by Frances Hodgson Burnett (author of The Secret Garden). An ollllllld classic which features a lot of rich people, some bad, some good, and an interesting reflection on the difference between Americans and Brits. A fun read, though I skimmed a great deal because really--those Edwardian descriptions. . . . !!!
    In the car I have been listening to Eugenides's Middlesex, which I got at Marden's (!!) and which is 17 disks long, and which is . . . interesting. Informative. Weird. Sprawling. Can't imagine why he decided to cobble all these bits together, but it's an interesting "listen". And for 6.99, I can't complain!

Mar. 2: Ha! Thanks, Snow Day. . .Finished Come and Tell me Some Lies and ended up enjoying it, though its structure was ragged and overly artsy. It jumped around through the narrator's life in what seemed like an effort to elevate it from autobiography to Literary Effort. Have now started A Perfect Life, the one I couldn't remember yesterday, and it's improved, though still lacks the charm of Hens Dancing and Summertime. Doesn't bode well for our longtime relationship, I fear.

Mar. 1: Just finished Enter Sir Robert, another Thirkell re-read. Got Come on and Tell me Some Lies by Raffaella Barker, as well as her second-newest (title escapes me) , but so far I think I like her earlier work much better. That's a disappointment, but maybe I'll change my mind!

Also finished listening to An Incomplete Revenge, a Maisie Dobbs novel by Jacqueline Winspear. I thought I'd read it, but I hadn't! A pleasure, as usual.

up to 2/11: Have just started a French classic, The Grand Estate, for an online book club, and realized I need to finish it by Saturday. It's good, so maybe I can!
    Slogged through Angela Thirkell's Happy Returns, tho it got better!, and then started rereading Raffaella Barker's Summertime, which I love, but perhaps these leisurely books set in upper crust British society are why I feel a little malaise in my present middle class teacher life!

2/10: whoosh. Update on "in car reading": Unaccustomed Earth: wow. Knocked my socks off, esp the last trilogy of connected stories. . .just as Lori Fineman, as I talked her ear off about it! Now listening to the Winspear. . . halfway through the first cd and already sucked in.

1/27: On DVD: Rex Stout's If Death Ever Slept--a lot of fun, though not terribly brilliant writing; now listening to Jumpa Lahiri's Unaccustomed Earth. I'm not usually a big fan of short stories, but her voice is wonderful. Next up will be Jacqueline Winspear's second-newest and then I will treat myself to Lauren Willig's newest foolishness!

And in actual print: Margaret Maron's Death's Half Acre--enjoyable; Will Thomas's Some Danger Involved, a very detailed historical detective story that was interesting but awkwardly written in places; Angela Thirkell's Love Among the Ruins--a reread to visit postwar Britain and a way of life I've never known!; Rosemary Sutcliffe's The Lantern Bearers: interesting ya historical fiction. Started well, then dragged. Ernest Gaines's A Lesson Before Dying--knocked my socks off again. Soon to come: The Lost Estate for an online book club!


1/13: Finished listening to Joan Bauer's Peeled; if I could've found a replacement book I'd've taken it, as Peeled was a weak novel, though it got more interesting as time went on. The plot was unrealistic, characterization quite weak, developments predictable. . . . Overall, I thought Bauer presumed on her audience's patience and good will. NOT a good book!

1/5: Let's see: over vacation I listened to Wintersmith on cd with the family; I reread Pilcher's Winter Solstice; read and loved  Belong to Me by Marisa de los Santos and finished the Terry Pratchett series (out of order) : Wee Free Men and Hat Made of Sky (both a lot of fun but not as good as Wintersmith). I also enjoyed reading Elizabeth Zimmerman's The Opinionated Knitter!

12/21: Finished Inkheart yesterday and ended up enjoying it quite a bit, tho the middle dragged. Right after I started Ann Hood's The Knitting Circle and then finished it today. It was very sad and a bit uneven, but a moving and inspirational story in many ways. It certainly makes me want to knit!
Note: vacation reading will be in purple, just to celebrate. 

12/15ish: Started Inkheart, by Cornelia Funke. I liked Thief Lord. This one is slower moving, but I like it pretty well at halfway through.

12/19: Just finished "Wintersmith" by Terry Prachett on cd. It was a huge pleasure--interesting ideas about the role of story and myth and human experience, but also laugh-out-loud funny in many places. I wish it could've been longer!
